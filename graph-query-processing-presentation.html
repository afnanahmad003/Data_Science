Understanding how we search and find information in connected data

Real-world analogy:

Think of it like searching through Facebook to find all your friends who work at Google and live in San Francisco

Graph vs Subgraph

Isomorphic Graphs:

Types of Graph Databases

1. Single Graph Database:

One big connected network

Example: All of Facebook - everyone is connected in one giant graph

2. Transactional Graph Database:

Many separate small graphs

Example: A chemistry database where each molecule is its own separate graph

What is Graph Query Processing?

Finding patterns or specific structures in graph data

Real-life Example:

You want to find: "All people who are friends with both Alice AND Bob"

The challenge: How to search through millions of connections quickly without checking every single person one by one?

The Problem:

Checking every possible connection is extremely slow (like reading every page in a library to find one book)

The Indexing Solution

Create shortcuts to find information faster

Library Analogy:

‚ùå Without Index: Walk through every shelf and check every book

‚úÖ With Index: Use the card catalog to jump directly to the right section

In Graphs:

Instead of checking all connections, we pre-organize the data so we can quickly eliminate large portions that don't match what we're looking for

Store information about all structures in the database

Like a Phone Book:

‚Ä¢ Lists EVERYONE in the city

‚Ä¢ Easy to update when someone moves

‚Ä¢ But takes forever to find "all plumbers in downtown"

Not very smart - doesn't prioritize important patterns

Stores all possible paths up to a certain length

Road Trip Example:

Imagine recording all possible driving routes between cities up to 3 stops:

‚Ä¢ NYC ‚Üí Boston ‚Üí Portland

‚Ä¢ LA ‚Üí Vegas ‚Üí Phoenix

‚Ä¢ Miami ‚Üí Atlanta ‚Üí Nashville

GD-Index Technique

Breaks big graphs into smaller connected pieces

Puzzle Analogy:

Take a 1000-piece jigsaw puzzle and organize it:

‚Ä¢ First by corner pieces

‚Ä¢ Then edge pieces

‚Ä¢ Then by color groups

This creates a hierarchy - when looking for a blue sky piece, you only check the blue section

Best for: Small to medium puzzles. Gets overwhelming with giant puzzles.

Identifies basic building block shapes in graphs

LEGO Blocks Example:

Instead of describing a LEGO castle piece by piece, you identify standard blocks:

‚Ä¢ Line: 4 blocks in a row

‚Ä¢ Star: 1 center block with 4 blocks attached

‚Ä¢ Cycle: Blocks forming a closed loop

Now you can say "Find me all castles with a star pattern" much faster

Limitation: Only works well for chemistry/molecules. Not great for social networks.

GraphREL Technique

Converts graphs into database tables

Spreadsheet Example:

Convert your social network into Excel:

‚Ä¢ Table 1: People (ID, Name, Age)

‚Ä¢ Table 2: Friendships (Person1, Person2)

Now you can use familiar database tools (like SQL) to search

Mining-Based Indexing

Smart Pattern Recognition

Only index patterns that appear frequently

Shopping Mall Example:

Instead of mapping every possible route through the mall:

‚Ä¢ Find the top 10 most-walked paths

‚Ä¢ Only create maps for those popular routes

‚Ä¢ "80% of shoppers go: Entrance ‚Üí Food Court ‚Üí Apple Store"

Advantage:

Much faster and more efficient for common searches

Indexes only frequently occurring subgraphs

A restaurant notices patterns:

‚Ä¢ 70% of customers order: Burger + Fries + Coke

‚Ä¢ 50% order: Pizza + Beer

‚Ä¢ Only 2% order: Salad + Water + Soup

They create "combo meals" for the popular patterns only. This makes ordering faster for most people.

Smart: If someone orders the rare combination, it takes longer but most queries are super fast

Uses tree structures (simpler than graphs) as index

Instead of mapping all possible relationships:

‚Ä¢ CEO ‚Üí VPs ‚Üí Directors ‚Üí Managers ‚Üí Employees

‚Ä¢ This tree structure is much simpler

‚Ä¢ Finding "all reports under a VP" is easy

Creates an inverted index of frequent closed patterns

Like the index at the back of a textbook:

‚Ä¢ "DNA" ‚Üí pages 45, 78, 102, 234

‚Ä¢ "Proteins" ‚Üí pages 89, 102, 156

‚Ä¢ "Cell Division" ‚Üí pages 12, 67, 234

Similarity Search Methods

Finding 'Close Enough' Matches

Sometimes exact matches aren't possible or necessary

Dating App Example:

You want to find someone:

‚Ä¢ Age: 25-30 (not exactly 27)

‚Ä¢ Likes: hiking, reading, cooking

‚Ä¢ Lives: within 20 miles

You don't need a PERFECT match - someone who's 31 and lives 22 miles away might be great too!

Why use it:

‚Ä¢ Data might be incomplete or messy

‚Ä¢ "Close enough" is good enough

‚Ä¢ Much faster than finding perfect matches

K-NNs and Range Queries

K-NNs (K-Nearest Neighbors):

"Find me the 5 closest matches"

Like Netflix saying "Here are your top 5 recommended movies"

Range Query:

"Find everything within a certain similarity score"

Like Zillow showing "All houses between $300K-$400K in your neighborhood"

Difference: K-NNs gives you a fixed number of results. Range query gives you ALL results that meet your criteria (could be 3, could be 300)

Edit Distance & MCS Distance

Edit Distance:

How many changes to transform one graph into another?

Like spell check: "cat" ‚Üí "car" = 1 change (t‚Üír)

In graphs: Add/remove people or friendships

MCS (Maximum Common Subgraph) Distance:

How much do two graphs overlap?

Like comparing two friend groups: "You and Sarah have 8 mutual friends out of 20 total"

Higher overlap = more similar graphs

‚Ä¢ Items 40000-49999 ‚Üí Aisle 4

‚Ä¢ Node Mismatches: Eye color different but close

‚Ä¢ Looking for "coffee shops near Times Square"? Only search that small neighborhood
